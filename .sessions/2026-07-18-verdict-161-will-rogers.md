# VERDICT 161 — Will Rogers phenomenon / stage migration: reclassifying members across a group boundary raises the mean of BOTH groups at once while the population mean is EXACTLY unchanged

Reproduce PROPOSAL 148 (round-34 UNRELATED slot, P148 → V161, +13): the Will Rogers phenomenon / stage migration. A population of N members each carries a FIXED real value v ~ Uniform[0,1] (read "normalised survival / quality — higher is better"). A COARSE classifier splits them at T_COARSE=0.5 into LOW (v < T_COARSE) and HIGH (v >= T_COARSE); HIGH's mean exceeds LOW's by construction. A FINER classifier then MIGRATES the bottom slice of HIGH — members with T_COARSE <= v < T_FINE=0.6 — DOWN into LOW (the "stage migration": borderline members reclassified into the group whose mean they beat). Nothing but the LABEL changes; the value multiset is never modified. The folk belief: "if the average outcome improves within EVERY subgroup, the population as a whole must have improved — you cannot make every part better without making the whole better." This is the intuition every stage-specific / segment-specific dashboard rests on. Reality (the Will Rogers phenomenon, named for the quip "when the Okies left Oklahoma and moved to California, they raised the average intelligence level in both states"): moving a member x from HIGH (higher mean) to LOW (lower mean), where LOW_mean < x < HIGH_mean, RAISES both group means at once — LOW loses nothing and gains an above-its-mean member, HIGH sheds a below-its-mean member — while the pooled mean is IDENTICALLY conserved, because the member was only RELABELLED and its value never touched. Every subgroup can improve while the whole does not move a hair. The serious form is the medical STAGE-MIGRATION artifact (Feinstein, Sosin & Wells, NEJM 1985): finer diagnostics reclassify borderline patients from an EARLY (better-prognosis) stage into a LATE (worse-prognosis) stage, and stage-specific survival rises in BOTH stages — because the migrated patients were the WORST of the early stage yet BETTER than the average late patient — even though not one patient's actual outcome changed. A model-dependence result (the P024 discipline): "every subgroup improved" and "the population improved" are DIFFERENT claims — they coincide only when group membership is FIXED; let the boundary move and both subgroup means can rise on a population that did not change at all. The verifier runs R=40 independent paired trials (per-trial seed SEED + r·TRIAL_STRIDE), each drawing N=4000 fixed values and computing the before/after group means plus the pooled mean identically before and after, and evaluates three ordered gates on the /se convention (z=mean/se, SIGMA_GATE=3.0): G1 AGREEMENT (two independent halves of the R low-group improvements agree within noise, |z|<3) → G2 CONSERVATION-CONTROL (the pooled mean does NOT improve — max relative pooled-mean change <= CONS_TOL=1e-9 with count conservation AND the pooled change is NOT significantly positive, z<3) → G3 HEAD (BOTH subgroup means rise significantly — the smaller of the LOW/HIGH improvement z-scores is >= SIGMA_GATE and both means are positive). On the pinned world (SEED=20260717) all three gates hold in order and the run is deterministic. The trap: an analyst who reads "every stage-specific survival improved" as "outcomes improved" is ignoring that a moving classification boundary can raise every subgroup mean while no member's value — and the population mean — changed at all.
