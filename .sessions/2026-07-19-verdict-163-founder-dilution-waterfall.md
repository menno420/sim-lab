ONE company raises RAISE=$10M under TWO term sheets evaluated on the SAME exit draw (paired) — Offer A (LOWER headline valuation, CLEAN terms: pre 30 → post 40, investor owns f_A=0.25, 1x NON-PARTICIPATING preferred) vs Offer B (HIGHER headline valuation, STACKED terms: pre 50 → post 60, investor owns f_B=1/6, 2x PARTICIPATING preferred, uncapped). Founder (common) proceeds at exit X: founder_A(X)=X−max(1x pref, f_A·X capped at X)=X−max(10, 0.25X) (non-participating: investor takes the GREATER of preference or as-converted common), founder_B(X)=(1−f_B)·max(0, X−2·pref)=(1−1/6)(X−20) for X>20 (participating: 2x preference off the top THEN pro-rata share of the remainder). The folk belief: "a higher pre-money valuation is unambiguously better for founders — same cash in, less dilution; between two offers raising the same money, take the higher valuation, the liquidation-preference fine print only bites in a fire sale." It is NOT: the two founder proceeds functions are equal at the analytic crossover X*=200 exactly (0.75X = 0.8333(X−20)), so BELOW $200M exit Offer A pays founders strictly more (the participation multiple dominates) and only extreme moonshots above X* reverse it — a participating multiple is a large FIRST-ORDER founder haircut while a headline-valuation bump is a small TAIL-ONLY credit, so the valuation number is nearly orthogonal to realised founder proceeds.
