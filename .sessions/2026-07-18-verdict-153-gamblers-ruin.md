The classical two-barrier first-passage law gives P(reach N before 0 | start i) = (1 − (q/p)^i) / (1 − (q/p)^N) for p ≠ 0.5, and the plain ratio i/N for the fair game p = 0.5. Folk belief (inverted here): "the house edge is tiny — a ~1.5% edge only shaves a percent or two off my chances, the penalty scales LINEARLY with the edge." FALSE. Write r = q/p; for p < 0.5 the ruin penalty is EXPONENTIAL in the target distance through the barrier ratio (q/p)^N, not linear in the edge — from a symmetric fair start (i=100, N=200) worth P = 0.5, nudging p from 0.500 to 0.485 (a 1.5% edge) collapses P to ~0.002466, roughly a 200× drop, NOT the ~0.485 a linear-in-the-edge intuition predicts. Mechanism: first-step analysis P_i = p·P_{i+1} + q·P_{i−1} with P_0=0, P_N=1 solves to the geometric form; with r>1 for p<0.5, from i=N/2 the reach probability P = 1/(1+r^{N/2}) ~ 1/(1+e^{ε·N}) is governed by the PRODUCT ε·N, so a fixed small edge produces an arbitrarily large collapse as N grows — the per-round disadvantages MULTIPLY through the barrier ratio, they do not add.
