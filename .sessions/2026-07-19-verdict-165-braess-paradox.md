ONE origin-destination pair (Start→End) carries a fixed driver population under TWO topologies evaluated on the SAME demand draw (paired) — Topology BASE (two parallel routes only: Start→A→End and Start→B→End, where Start→A and B→End are CONGESTIBLE edges with cost x/CAP in the flow x across them and A→End, Start→B are CONSTANT-latency links) vs Topology SHORTCUT (BASE plus one extra A→B edge of ZERO cost, opening the new route Start→A→B→End that chains BOTH congestible edges). At the selfish/Wardrop user equilibrium each driver minimises their own travel time given everyone else's routing; the folk belief: "adding a road can only help — a new zero-cost link is a strictly dominant option that at worst goes unused, so total and per-driver travel time can only fall or stay flat; tearing out a road to speed traffic up is absurd." It is NOT: above a demand threshold ≈3000 (30·CAP=100) the zero-cost shortcut is used by EVERY driver at equilibrium (the chained-congestible route strictly dominates each parallel route for each individual), which drives BOTH congestible edges to full population flow and raises the equilibrium travel time for everyone — a strictly benevolent edge makes the selfish equilibrium strictly WORSE, and deleting it restores the faster equilibrium.
