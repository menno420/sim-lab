## Claim reproduced

Mock Turtles — turn 1, 2, or 3 coins, rightmost turned coin Heads→Tails, normal play (last to move wins) — has single-coin Sprague–Grundy value `g(n) = ` the unique odious member of `{2n, 2n+1}` (`= 2n` if popcount(n) is odd, else `2n+1`), the odious numbers 1,2,4,7,8,11,13,14,16,… (OEIS A000069, so `g(n)` is the `n`-th odious number). By the coin-turning decomposition (a corollary of the Sprague–Grundy sum theorem — every coin-turning position is the disjunctive sum of its single-heads coins) the Grundy value of any position with heads at set `S` is the nim-sum `⊕_{n∈S} g(n)`, so the first player wins iff that nim-sum is nonzero and the P-positions are exactly those with nim-sum 0. The verifier proves this firsthand by an independent mex-DP game oracle (memoized over frozenset positions; a legal move toggles `{h} ∪ (any 0/1/2 indices below head h)`, `grundy(∅)=0`) that knows nothing of the closed form: the single-coin DP matches the odious formula for `n=0..255` and the general oracle matches the XOR-sum over all `2^13` positions (G1); an iid Monte-Carlo agrees with the exact P-density `p0 = 1/32` at `z = −1.7597` (G2); the oracle is XOR-additive across disjoint coordinate blocks and the closed-form strategy never loses (G3); and the two most plausible wrong value-rules — Turning Turtles' `g=n` (plain Nim) and the un-corrected `g=2n+1` — are rejected at the Grundy-value level at `z = 7832.71` and `z = 1413.69` (G4). The natural foil is Turning Turtles (turn 1 OR 2 coins → `g(n)=n`); allowing three coins is exactly what introduces the odious correction.
